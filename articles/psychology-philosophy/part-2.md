Most people believe they understand how everyday objects work, a zipper, a toilet, a bicycle, far better than they actually do. Ask someone to explain the mechanism in detail and the confident answer usually collapses into vague gestures and hand-waving.

This gap between felt understanding and actual understanding has a name: the illusion of explanatory depth. It thrives because we rarely test our knowledge by trying to articulate it, we simply borrow the fluency of familiarity and mistake it for comprehension.

The same illusion shapes political opinion. People hold strong views on complex policy yet, when asked to explain the underlying mechanism step by step, their confidence quietly deflates.

## Word Bank

### mechanism
**Meaning:** a system of parts working together, or the process by which something happens

**Example:** The mechanism behind inflation is more complex than most headlines suggest.

### articulate
**Meaning:** to express an idea clearly and effectively in words

**Example:** She struggled to articulate why the plan felt wrong.

### fluency
**Meaning:** the quality of being smooth and effortless, especially in speech or thought

**Example:** His fluency in the topic came from years of casual reading, not deep study.

### deflates
**Meaning:** loses confidence, air, or importance, often suddenly

**Example:** His certainty deflates the moment he's asked a follow-up question.
